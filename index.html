01 — The gut microbiome

A complex, indispensable ecosystem

The human gut harbours a vast microbial consortium of hundreds of bacterial, archaeal, and eukaryotic species. In the large intestine, the community is dominated by the phyla Bacteroidota and Bacillota, which together account for roughly 90% of microbial abundance.[1]

A central function of the gut microbiota is the fermentation of complex dietary fibres into short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), which contribute almost 10% of host energy requirements.[3] Early ODE-based frameworks, such as the Muñoz-Tamayo model, established the "trophic chain" of this fermentation process — but assume constant fibre intake and lump all carbohydrates into a single variable, diverging significantly from reality.

02 — The washout problem

Cremer's biophysical models

From a fluid dynamics perspective, the gut is a tube with constant forward flow. If bacteria were simply suspended in luminal content, they would be expelled before they could reproduce — a phenomenon termed washout.

Cremer et al.[4] constructed a 2D partial differential model that showed peristaltic mixing is necessary to combat washout. Their model considers flow to peak an hour after food has been consumed, with a dynamic radius accounting for gut contractions. A dead-end cecal pouch provides a region safe from flow where bacterial populations can be replenished.

Salari & Cremer[5] extended this by incorporating diurnal cecal volume dynamics, showing daily population bottlenecks of $N_e \sim 10^7$–$10^{11}$ cells driven by meal-driven washout events.

Cremer et al., PNAS 2016 — Spatial population dynamics [4]
$$\frac{\partial \rho(x,t)}{\partial t} = \underbrace{\lambda(x,t)\,\rho}_{\text{growth}} \;-\; \underbrace{v(x,t)\,\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial x}}_{\text{advection (flow)}} \;+\; \underbrace{D\,\frac{\partial^2 \rho}{\partial x^2}}_{\text{diffusion}}$$
$\rho(x,t)$ — bacterial density along the intestinal axis $x$ over time. $\lambda(x,t)\rho$ — Growth: local net growth rate governed by nutrient availability; can become negative during strong flow events. $v(x,t)\,\partial_x \rho$ — Advection: fluid flow transports bacteria downstream, driving washout following meals. $D\,\partial^2_x \rho$ — Diffusion: effective mixing from peristalsis, enabling redistribution and recolonisation.
03 — Our biological system

B. theta feeds B. breve

Our model investigates gut community structure driven by cross-feeding between primary degraders and secondary consumers. Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron ($B_p$) cleaves complex inulin into fructo-oligosaccharides (FOS), which Bifidobacterium breve ($B_c$) then consumes, as it lacks the enzymes for initial polymer breakdown.

Growth in this system follows Monod kinetics, where the rate depends on substrate availability via the half-saturation constant $K_s$. This constant represents the substrate concentration at which growth reaches half of its maximum value. Crucially, $K_s$ differs across species and substrates. This asymmetry in substrate affinity reinforces the directional nature of cross-feeding, where primary degraders generate accessible metabolites that selectively sustain secondary consumers. [6] [7]

04 — Our approach

Our approach

We developed a time-dependent ODE model that replaces traditional steady-state assumptions with a dynamic meal influx function $M(t)$. Using the Ashoka University canteen menu, we simulated a 10-day dietary profile by randomly selecting three food items and one beverage for each of breakfast, lunch, and dinner.

Nutrient delivery was characterised using discrete Gaussian distributions. Unlike models that assume instantaneous spikes or constant flow, this accounts for the gradual passage of a meal through the colon — fibre concentration increases toward a peak and gradually dissipates, with a 1.5h standard deviation centred 1.5h after each meal.

The Model

Model components
Four interacting state variables

We use a system of coupled ODEs to model the rate of change for two bacterial populations and two nutrients in the proximal colon, treated as a Continuous Stirred-Tank Reactor (CSTR).

Reactor geometry
CSTR

The proximal colon is modelled as an open, well-mixed system with inflow and outflow, justified by peristaltic contraction mixing.[3]

Growth kinetics
Monod + logistic crowding

Each species grows on its limiting substrate with species-specific $\lambda_0$ and $K$. A shared logistic crowding term prevents unbounded growth.

Cross-feeding link
Inulin → FOS coupling

Fraction $\varepsilon_c = 0.45$ of inulin consumed by B. theta converts to FOS. This is B. breve's sole carbon source, making cross-feeding obligate and directional.[6]

Dietary input
Meal function M(t)

Gaussian pulses centred 1.5h after each meal. Amplitude computed from per-item dietary fibre via inulin fraction (0.15) and MW (504 g/mol).

Governing equations
The coupled ODE system

Four state variables are tracked over time $t$: bacterial concentrations $B_p$ (B. theta) and $B_c$ (B. breve), inulin concentration $N_I$, and FOS concentration $N_{FOS}$. Each equation decomposes into labelled biological terms.

Bacterial populations — Growth · Crowding · Washout
1
$$\frac{dB_p}{dt} = \underbrace{\left(\lambda_p \frac{N_I}{K_p + N_I}\right) B_p}_{\text{Monod growth}} \cdot \underbrace{\left(1 - \frac{B_p s_p + B_c s_c}{S}\right)}_{\text{Logistic crowding}} - \underbrace{\frac{1}{\tau} B_p}_{\text{Washout}}$$
2
$$\frac{dB_c}{dt} = \underbrace{\left(\lambda_c \frac{N_{FOS}}{K_c + N_{FOS}}\right) B_c}_{\text{Monod growth}} \cdot \underbrace{\left(1 - \frac{B_p s_p + B_c s_c}{S}\right)}_{\text{Logistic crowding}} - \underbrace{\frac{1}{\tau} B_c}_{\text{Washout}}$$
Monod growth: $\lambda_p$ or $\lambda_c$ is the species max growth rate; $K_p$/$K_c$ is the Monod (half-saturation) constant — substrate concentration at which growth is half-maximal. Logistic crowding: $(B_p s_p + B_c s_c)/S$ is total niche occupancy relative to carrying capacity $S$. Growth collapses to zero as the lumen fills. Washout: $1/\tau$ is the colonic clearance rate; luminal contents (bacteria and nutrients) are continuously removed at this rate.
Nutrient dynamics — Meal intake · Uptake by $B_p$ · Production / Consumption · Washout
3
$$\frac{dN_I}{dt} = \underbrace{M(t)}_{\text{Meal intake}} - \underbrace{\frac{\lambda_p}{Y_p} \frac{N_I}{K_p + N_I} B_p}_{\text{Uptake by }B_p} - \underbrace{\frac{1}{\tau_N} N_I}_{\text{Washout}}$$
4
$$\frac{dN_{FOS}}{dt} = \underbrace{U_1 \frac{\lambda_p}{Y_p} \frac{N_I}{K_p + N_I} B_p \cdot \varepsilon_c}_{\text{Production from inulin}} - \underbrace{\frac{\lambda_c}{Y_c} \frac{N_{FOS}}{K_c + N_{FOS}} B_c \cdot U_{FOS}}_{\text{Consumption by }B_c} - \underbrace{\frac{1}{\tau_N} N_{FOS}}_{\text{Washout}}$$
$M(t)$ — Meal intake: Gaussian pulse centred 1.5 h after each meal (08:00, 13:00, 20:00). Amplitude = fibre (g) × 0.15 / (inulin MW 504 g/mol × colon volume 0.377 L), giving mM h⁻¹. $\lambda/Y$ — Uptake: $Y_p$ and $Y_c$ are yield coefficients (CFU per mM substrate). The ratio $\lambda/Y$ converts growth into nutrient consumption rate. Eq. 4 cross-feeding link: $\varepsilon_c = 0.45$ of the inulin consumed by B. theta becomes FOS — B. breve's sole carbon source. $U_1 = U_{FOS} = 1.0$ (full uptake). RK4, $dt = 0.001$ h, 240,000 steps.

Assumptions
A1
Well-mixed lumen (CSTR)

The proximal colon is modelled as a Continuous Stirred-Tank Reactor. Peristaltic contractions justify this approximation, eliminating spatial gradients.

A2
Constant washout rate

$1/\tau = 0.0357$ h⁻¹ is held fixed. This ensures population dynamics are driven by nutrient availability rather than fluctuating transit speeds.

A3
Substrate specificity

B. theta is the sole primary degrader of inulin; B. breve grows exclusively on produced FOS. All substrate encountered by a cell is consumed — no partial leakage.

A4
Shared space constraints

Growth is limited by finite physical space $S$. The logistic crowding term applies to the combined niche occupancy of both species.

A5
Environmental stability

pH (~6.3) and temperature (37°C) are constant. Fermentation-driven acidification and its feedback on growth rates are not modelled.

A6
Luminal focus

Only free-swimming (luminal) populations are tracked. Mucus-attached or biofilm-associated bacteria are excluded.

Results

Simulations run over 10 days (240h) using the Ashoka University mess menu as dietary input, integrated with RK4 ($dt = 0.001$ h, 240,000 steps). Four panels characterise the nutrient and bacterial dynamics.

Figure 1
Nutrient concentrations over 10 days
Inference

We hypothesised oscillatory behaviour here: once inulin enters the gut, it is consumed by B. theta over time (producing the dip in the curve) before the next meal restores it. Analogous to a prey-predator model, FOS concentration should not peak before inulin's. However, the observed result shows FOS concentration continuously declining rather than oscillating. This is consistent with $Y_p$ being set too high and the initial $B_p$ population being too small to efficiently convert inulin to FOS, leaving the consumer substrate-starved across the simulation window.

Figure 2
Bacterial population dynamics over 10 days
Inference

We expected oscillatory coexistence: producer bacteria rising first as inulin becomes available, consumer populations then rising as FOS accumulates, followed by joint decline as nutrients are exhausted — the cycle repeating at each meal. Instead, the simulation shows B. theta rising exponentially, while B. breve remains suppressed near zero. Nutrients are constantly replenished from three daily meals, preventing the boom-bust cycles we anticipated. The large divergence in population sizes may stem from a high $Y_p$ generating disproportionate B. theta concentration per unit inulin consumed, or from washout rates that differentially disadvantage the consumer.

Figure 3
Phase portrait: substrate dynamics (FOS vs Inulin)
Inference

The nutrient phase portrait reveals clear producer-consumer coupling: FOS cannot increase before inulin does, and concentrations for both drop as they are utilised by their respective bacteria. The time-coloured trajectory shows the system initially transiting from high-FOS / low-inulin (start, top left) toward increasing inulin from meals, with FOS gradually declining to near zero by the end of the simulation. The tight convergence of late-time trajectories near the inulin axis confirms FOS depletion — a signature of the $Y_p$ mismatch identified in Figure 1.

Figure 4
Phase portrait: bacterial co-dynamics (B. breve vs B. theta)
Inference

We expected coexistence behaviour, with both consumer and producer populations cycling together in a limit cycle. The phase portrait shows instead a monotonic trajectory: B. theta grows continuously along the x-axis while B. breve collapses from its initial value toward zero. This is consistent with competitive exclusion driven by the near-zero FOS supply — the cross-feeding chain is effectively broken by insufficient FOS production, leaving B. breve unable to maintain its population above the washout threshold.

Discussion

Scenario 2 — Varying growth-to-yield ratios ($\lambda/Y$)
How $\lambda/Y$ dictates competition and coexistence

When the producer ratio $\lambda_p/Y_p$ is high due to high growth: B. theta plateaus quickly, but B. breve faces early extinction from initial FOS scarcity. Lowering $Y_p$ to $10^5$ rescues the consumer, allowing it to overtake B. theta; at $Y_p = 10^3$, B. breve enters exponential growth as FOS production spikes. A low producer ratio with low growth causes B. theta growth to flatten and eventually crash, while high yield increases B. theta rapidly but leaves B. breve unable to persist. For the consumer, a high $\lambda_c/Y_c$ ratio slows B. breve decay under high growth conditions, while low yield accelerates it. Under specific parameter combinations, both populations coexist exponentially as FOS builds up. Low consumer growth leads to total extinction of B. breve, while high yield enables faster recovery.

Scenario 3 — Varying residence time ($\tau$)
Sensitivity to bacterial and nutrient washout

Decreasing $\tau_b$ (bacterial washout) was expected to deplete B. theta, but high inulin levels prevent a crash. However, B. breve drops to near zero as FOS production cannot offset the increased removal rate. Decreasing $\tau_n$ (nutrient washout) does not deplete inulin but increases its oscillation amplitude. High yield minimises the consumption impact on the nutrient pool, causing the $N/t$ term to increase despite faster turnover. Notably, low yield conditions produce the most interesting dynamics: coexistence and nutrient oscillation emerge, visible in phase portraits. Varying $\varepsilon_c$ and $Y_p$ together produces detailed bifurcation-like behaviours worth further investigation.

Caveats
Where the model falls short

Several important biological realities are not captured. The model treats the proximal colon as a single well-mixed compartment — missing the spatial gradients a PDE framework would resolve.[4] The mucus layer is entirely absent.[1] pH dynamics are not modelled — fermentation-driven acidification would differentially affect species growth rates. The simplifying assumption $U_1 = U_{FOS} = 1.0$ may overestimate FOS availability. The 15% inulin fraction of dietary fibre applied here is a literature average and may vary significantly meal-to-meal.

Future directions
What else can be done
01
Spatial PDE extension

Combine the Cremer advection-diffusion framework[4] with our metabolic ODE system for a spatially resolved, cross-feeding PDE model.

02
Diurnal cecal dynamics

Incorporate cecal expansion-contraction cycles from Salari & Cremer (2025)[5] to add realistic volume dynamics to meal-driven inflow.

03
Resolved fibre types

Replace the single inulin pool with distinct substrate classes (resistant starch, pectin, arabinoxylan, long-chain inulin) each with different kinetics.[3]

04
pH and SCFA feedback

Couple SCFA production to a dynamic pH model, introducing negative feedback on growth rates and predicting the proximal-to-distal pH gradient.

05
Multi-species community

Extend to 6-8 species by adding F. prausnitzii, A. muciniphila, and methanogens to test whether cross-feeding cascades maintain community stability.[6]

06
Experimental validation

Test predictions in a flow-cell co-culture of B. theta and B. breve with controlled inulin inputs — the essential step toward making the model falsifiable.

Modelling journey
Five iterations from PDE to real meals

The final model emerged through successive iterations, each adding one layer of biological realism while keeping the system analytically tractable.

Iteration 01
Spatial Foundation (Cremer)
We began with the Cremer advection-diffusion PDE model, tracking bacteria and nutrients across space and time using Navier-Stokes equations with varying viscosity. Showed peristaltic mixing essential for population survival.
Iteration 02
Dimensional reduction to ODEs
To focus on long-term dietary effects, spatial PDEs were simplified to ODEs. Advection and diffusion assumed constant; Monod kinetics retained to keep growth nutrient-dependent.
Iteration 03
Cross-feeding trophic chain
Expanded to two species. Introduced the inulin-FOS link: fraction $\varepsilon_c = 0.1$ of processed inulin liberated as FOS, creating obligate metabolic dependency between producer and consumer.
Iteration 04
Space constraints & washout
Added shared logistic crowding to prevent unbounded population explosions, and a constant washout term for both bacteria and nutrients representing continuous natural clearance.
✓ Final model
Ashoka menu as M(t)
Replaced constant inflow with Gaussian meal pulses derived from real dietary fibre data from the Ashoka canteen. 10-day feast-and-famine cycles, calculating how specific student meal patterns dictate microbiome survival.

Each iteration added exactly one degree of realism. The final model is the minimal system that simultaneously captures CSTR geometry, inulin→FOS cross-feeding, logistic crowding, and real dietary inflow.
