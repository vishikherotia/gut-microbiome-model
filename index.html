01 — The gut microbiome

A complex, indispensable ecosystem

The human gut harbours a vast microbial community composed of hundreds of bacterial species alongside archaea and eukaryotes. In the large intestine, the community is dominated by the phyla Bacteroidota and Bacillota, accounting for roughly 90% of microbial abundance in most individuals.[1]

The gut is spatially stratified across its longitudinal (stomach to colon) and transverse (lumen to mucus to epithelium) axes. Gradients of pH, oxygen tension, antimicrobial peptides, and flow rate drive distinct community compositions at each location.[1][2]

The microbiota degrades complex dietary polysaccharides, producing short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) that contribute up to 10% of host energy requirements.[3] Dysbiosis is linked to IBD, colorectal cancer, obesity, and neurological conditions.

02 — Model framework

A biophysical model of growth, flow, and transport

To understand microbial dynamics in the proximal colon, Salari & Cremer[5] model the gut as a flowing, reactive system where microbial growth is tightly coupled to nutrient transport and fluid dynamics.

The system is described by coupled partial differential equations capturing: advection (flow), diffusion (mixing), and growth (nutrient-limited).

$$ \frac{\partial c_b}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot (D \nabla c_b - u c_b) + \lambda \frac{c_n}{c_n + K} c_b $$

Microbial dynamics: transport by flow + diffusion, with growth governed by Monod kinetics.

$$ \frac{\partial c_n}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot (D \nabla c_n - u c_n) - \frac{\lambda}{Y} \frac{c_n}{c_n + K} c_b $$

Nutrient dynamics: nutrients are transported similarly but are depleted by microbial consumption.

$$ \rho \left( \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + (u \cdot \nabla)u \right) = -\nabla p + \mu \nabla^2 u $$

Flow: fluid motion drives transport and is modeled using hydrodynamics.

Key assumptions

  • Pipe-like geometry of the proximal colon
  • No bacterial attachment to intestinal walls
  • Mixing approximated as diffusion
  • Growth limited by nutrient availability (Monod kinetics)

03 — Our biological system

B. theta feeds B. breve

Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron is a primary degrader possessing 88 Sus-like polysaccharide utilisation systems, enabling it to break down complex dietary fibres — including inulin — into smaller fructo-oligosaccharides (FOS).[2]

Bifidobacterium breve, a member of the Bifidobacterium genus, lacks the machinery to degrade large inulin polymers directly but is highly efficient at consuming the FOS oligosaccharides released by B. theta.[6][7] This cross-feeding relationship is a cornerstone of colonic ecology.

The fractional efficiency $\varepsilon_c = 0.45$ quantifies the proportion of inulin consumed by B. theta that is converted to FOS available for B. breve — making meal composition the primary driver of this interaction.

04 — Our approach

Modelling the gut as a living reactor

We model the proximal colon as a Continuous Stirred-Tank Reactor (CSTR) — an open system with constant inflow and outflow. Four coupled ODEs describe the dynamics of two bacterial species and two substrates, integrated using a 4th-order Runge-Kutta method over 10 days.

The nutrient inflow function $M(t)$ is derived directly from dietary fibre data extracted from the Ashoka University canteen menu using HealthifyMe — ten days of real meals, itemised to the individual food item, generating Gaussian pulses at each meal time.

This lets us ask: do real-world eating habits act as a growth filter on the microbiome — selecting for certain species over others, meal by meal?

02

The Model

Modelling journey

The final model emerged through five successive iterations, each adding one layer of biological realism while keeping the system analytically tractable.

Iteration 01
Single-species chemostat
Well-mixed reactor, constant glucose inflow, one species, Monod kinetics. Established washout criterion: growth rate must exceed dilution rate.
Iteration 02
Cremer advection-diffusion
Spatial PDEs along channel length. Peristaltic mixing (D) shown essential. Three regimes identified via dimensionless α = v²/(Dλ₀).
Iteration 03
Two-substrate CSTR
Switched to CSTR geometry. Introduced inulin N₁ and FOS Nᴫₒₛ as distinct substrates. B. theta releases FOS via fractional efficiency εc.
Iteration 04
Two-species + crowding
Added B. breve growing on FOS. Added logistic crowding term — both species compete for finite colonic niche S. Species ratio depends on meal composition.
✓ Final model
Ashoka menu as M(t)
Replaced constant inflow with Gaussian meal pulses derived from real dietary fibre data from the Ashoka University canteen. 10-day multi-meal simulation via RK4.

Each iteration added exactly one degree of realism. The final model is the minimal system that simultaneously captures CSTR reactor geometry, inulin→FOS cross-feeding, logistic crowding, and real dietary inflow.

Governing equations
The coupled ODE system

Four state variables are tracked over time $t$ in the proximal colon (CSTR): bacterial biomasses $B_p$ (B. theta) and $B_c$ (B. breve), inulin concentration $N_I$, and FOS concentration $N_{FOS}$.

Bacterial populations
1
$$\frac{dB_p}{dt} = \lambda_p \frac{N_I}{K_p + N_I} \cdot B_p \cdot \left(1 - \frac{B_p + B_c}{S}\right) - \frac{1}{\tau} B_p$$
2
$$\frac{dB_c}{dt} = \lambda_c \frac{N_{FOS}}{K_c + N_{FOS}} \cdot B_c \cdot \left(1 - \frac{B_p + B_c}{S}\right) - \frac{1}{\tau} B_c$$
Growth (Monod) · Logistic crowding toward carrying capacity S · Washout at rate 1/τ
Nutrient dynamics
3
$$\frac{dN_I}{dt} = M(t) - \frac{\lambda_p}{Y_p} \frac{N_I}{K_p + N_I} B_p - \frac{1}{\tau} N_I$$
4
$$\frac{dN_{FOS}}{dt} = \frac{\lambda_p}{Y_p} \frac{N_I}{K_p + N_I} B_p \cdot \varepsilon_c - \frac{\lambda_c}{Y_c} \frac{N_{FOS}}{K_c + N_{FOS}} B_c - \frac{1}{\tau} N_{FOS}$$
$M(t)$ = Gaussian meal pulse at 08:00, 13:00, 20:00 derived from Ashoka menu CSV (mM h⁻¹) Eq. 4 is the cross-feeding link: the $\varepsilon_c$ term produces FOS from inulin consumed by B. theta — B. breve’s sole carbon source $U_1 = U_{FOS} = 1.0$ (uptake efficiency, simplifying assumption) · RK4 integration, $dt = 0.001$ h
Assumptions
A1
Well-mixed lumen

The proximal colon is treated as a CSTR with no spatial gradients. Peristaltic contractions justify this approximation for the proximal segment.

A2
Constant washout rate

$1/ au = 0.0357$ h⁻¹ is held fixed. Peak diurnal washout rates would exceed both species' growth rates, causing artefactual washout at every meal.

A3
Full substrate uptake

Uptake efficiencies $U_1 = U_{FOS} = 1.0$. All substrate encountered by a bacterium is consumed — no partial uptake or leakage is modelled.

A4
Equal niche occupancy

$s_p = s_c = 1.0$ — both species occupy equal niche space per CFU. The carrying capacity $S$ is shared symmetrically between the two populations.

A5
Constant pH

pH is held constant at ~6.3 throughout the simulation. Fermentation-driven acidification and its feedback on growth rates are not modelled.

A6
Luminal bacteria only

Mucus-associated and wall-attached bacteria are neglected. The model describes only the luminal (free-swimming) population of each species.

Model components
Reactor geometry
CSTR

The proximal colon is modelled as a continuous stirred-tank reactor — an open, well-mixed system with inflow and outflow, justified by peristaltic contraction mixing.[3]

Growth kinetics
Monod + logistic crowding

Each species grows on its limiting substrate with species-specific $\lambda_0$ and $K$. A shared logistic crowding term prevents unbounded growth toward carrying capacity $S$.

Cross-feeding link
Inulin → FOS coupling

Fraction $\varepsilon_c = 0.45$ of inulin consumed by B. theta converts to FOS. This is B. breve’s sole carbon source, making cross-feeding obligate and directional.

Dietary input
Meal function M(t)

Gaussian pulses centred 1 h after each meal (gut transit delay). Amplitude computed from per-item dietary fibre content via inulin fraction (0.40) and MW (504 g/mol).

Discussion

What our model establishes
Diet acts as a selective filter on the microbiome

Most modelling frameworks treat nutrient input as constant or use compositionally fixed meal pulses. Our model demonstrates that the fibre content of individual meals — varying day to day in the Ashoka canteen — creates distinct microbial dynamics that a steady-state model cannot capture. High-fibre meals produce stronger B. breve growth pulses via elevated FOS production; low-fibre days suppress B. breve while B. theta maintains biomass near the carrying capacity. The meal menu acts as a real-time selective filter on the microbial community.

Research gaps filled
Bridging metabolic ecology and real dietary data

Prior models either captured physical flow[4][5] or metabolic trophic chains[3], but none integrated a real dietary input time series with explicit two-species cross-feeding dynamics. Our model fills this gap by: (i) using a real university canteen menu as $M(t)$; (ii) resolving the inulin→FOS metabolic pathway explicitly; (iii) including logistic crowding absent from purely nutrient-limited models; and (iv) providing an interactive simulator for parameter sensitivity exploration.

Caveats
Where the model falls short

Several important biological realities are not captured. The model treats the proximal colon as a single well-mixed compartment — missing the spatial gradients that a PDE framework would resolve.[4] The mucus layer is absent; the distinct mucosal community is entirely neglected.[1] pH dynamics are not modelled — fermentation-driven acidification would differentially affect species growth rates. The simplifying assumptions $U_1 = U_{FOS} = 1.0$ may overestimate FOS availability. Finally, B. breve and B. theta are represented as monolithic populations without within-species strain variation.

Future directions
What else can be done to model the gut microbiome
01
Spatial PDE extension

Combine the Cremer advection-diffusion framework[4] with our metabolic ODE system to produce a spatially resolved, cross-feeding PDE model of the proximal colon.

02
Diurnal cecal dynamics

Incorporate the cecal expansion-contraction and mass movement cycles from Salari & Cremer (2025)[5] to add realistic volume dynamics to the meal-driven inflow.

03
Resolved fibre types

Replace the single inulin pool with distinct substrate classes (resistant starch, pectin, arabinoxylan, long-chain inulin) each with different kinetics, as suggested by Muñoz-Tamayo.[3]

04
pH and SCFA feedback

Couple SCFA production to a dynamic pH model, introducing negative feedback on growth rates and allowing prediction of the proximal-to-distal pH gradient as a function of diet.

05
Multi-species community

Extend to 6–8 species by adding F. prausnitzii, A. muciniphila, Ruminococcus gnavus, and methanogens to test whether cross-feeding cascades maintain community stability.[6]

06
Experimental validation

Test predictions in a flow-cell co-culture of B. theta and B. breve with controlled inulin inputs — the essential step toward making the model falsifiable and predictive.
